AI Voice Simulation with DDSP

A continuing experiment in personal voice modeling, dataset construction, and controllable AI singing voice synthesis.

Status · In Progress Field · AI Audio Tool · DDSP Type · Experimental System

Overview

This project explores AI-based voice simulation through DDSP. Across three rounds of experimentation, the work has moved from early model training based on archived voice recordings to a more deliberate workflow involving dataset screening, updated model versions, and newly recorded first-hand audio material.

Background

The project began as an exploration of whether previously recorded voice materials could be used to construct a usable personal voice model. The earliest dataset came from old voice acting recordings. Those materials were not originally recorded for machine learning, but they provided a practical starting point for testing DDSP-based voice modeling.

The first version used an early DDSP workflow. The exact number of training steps was not fully recorded, but it was likely in the range of several tens of thousands, roughly around forty to fifty thousand steps. The result was sufficient for basic voice conversion experiments, especially for replacing the vocal tone of existing songs with a voice closer to my own.

Development Log

Phase I · Early DDSP Experiment

The first round used an early version of DDSP and a dataset assembled from old voice acting recordings. The training process was exploratory, with an estimated scale of forty to fifty thousand steps. This phase mainly tested whether the available audio could produce a recognizable personal voice model.

Phase II · DDSP-barbara-6.1

The second round used the same general source material, but the recordings were screened more carefully before training. This version was trained with DDSP-barbara-6.1 for 110,000 steps. Compared with the first version, the workflow became more controlled, and the model was used more regularly for song-based voice replacement experiments.

Phase III · Current Dataset and Updated Model

The current round is still in progress. It is planned around a newer model and a more deliberate dataset-building process. In addition to earlier materials, I am collecting first-hand voice recordings while playing games and speaking through voice chat. Since ordinary speech may not cover a wide enough pitch range, I am also considering recording selected songs as supplementary source material.

Method and Materials

The current workflow treats dataset construction as a central part of the project. Instead of relying only on archived voice clips, the newer phase emphasizes source control: clearer recordings, more consistent vocal material, and a broader range of pitch and expression.

Game voice-chat recordings provide natural speech samples and capture a more spontaneous speaking style. However, they may be limited in pitch coverage, recording consistency, and vocal stability. For that reason, sung material may be added later to improve coverage of high and low registers.

Application

Previous models were mainly used for experimental song conversion. The basic process was to take an existing song and transform the vocal timbre toward a model trained on my own voice. The purpose was not only to produce a novelty effect, but also to understand how training quality, dataset selection, and model version affect the final vocal texture.

In its current form, the project remains a personal technical experiment rather than a finished audio product. The focus is on model behavior, dataset quality, and the boundary between voice resemblance and controllable synthesis.

Future Direction

A later research direction is to experiment with cryptography- inspired modification of the model itself. Conceptually, if a trained model can be regarded as a structured sequence of numerical parameters, then selected numerical changes may be introduced to examine whether the model can be altered, watermarked, weakened, protected, or made more personally identifiable.

This idea is still speculative. Its feasibility has not yet been evaluated, and it would require a clearer understanding of model structure, parameter sensitivity, and the relationship between numerical modification and audible output. At the present stage, it is best understood as a possible experimental direction rather than a confirmed technical route.

This is an active project. The model, dataset, and technical route are still being revised as new recordings are collected and tested.
